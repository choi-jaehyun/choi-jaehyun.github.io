Working Papers

Migration Responses to State Abortion Policy

with Teresa Molina and Nicole Siegal PDF
IZA DP No. 18615

This paper examines whether U.S. interstate migration responds to state abortion policy. Using 2006-2019 data on gestational age limits and bilateral migration flows, we estimate gravity models with origin- and destination-state policies, economic, demographic, and political controls, and state-pair and year fixed effects. We find that gestational age limits do not significantly affect out-migration, but they reduce in-migration: individuals are less likely to move to states that adopt 20-week limits, the most restrictive policy observed during the study period. Effects are similar for men and women and are large among women past reproductive age, suggesting that migration responses are driven partly by ideological preferences rather than only anticipated need for abortion access. Results are robust to an extended two-way fixed effects estimator designed for heterogeneous treatment effects under staggered adoption in nonlinear models.

Peer and Place Effects on Fertility: Evidence from the U.S. Military Duty-Station Assignments

with Laura Armey, Ryan Sullivan, and Coady Wing

Low fertility has emerged as a defining demographic challenge globally. Even within the United States, where overall fertility has trended downward, fertility rates exhibit substantial geographic heterogeneity. These wide and persistent differences raise a question: to what extent do they reflect causal effects of place, rather than compositional sorting or other socioeconomic factors? We leverage quasi-random military duty-station assignments to study place and peer effects on fertility. Linking duty-station histories to annual fertility outcomes, dependent-status records, and marital transitions, we estimate how exposure to different local family environments affects subsequent fertility, marriage, and divorce. Our design uses commuting-zone indicators and inverse-propensity score weights to adjust for branch- and occupation-based assignment patterns. We estimate gender-specific place effects and examine marriage and divorce as related behavioral margins, providing causal evidence on whether local norms, family structures, and community environments shape fertility decisions.

Contraception Responses to Abortion Bans

with Mallory Dreyer, Hailemichael Shone, Nicole Siegal, Kosali Simon, and Coady Wing

We examine whether state abortion bans after Dobbs changed prescription contraception use among commercially insured women. Using longitudinal claims data and a stacked difference-in-differences design, we study contraception initiation among prior nonusers and switching from short-acting to long-acting methods among existing users, including heterogeneity by distance to the nearest abortion facility. Despite a gradual nationwide decline in contraception use, we find no evidence that post-Dobbs restrictions increased take-up or shifted women toward long-acting methods.

Ozempic Babies? The Effects of GLP-1 Agonists on Fertility

with Sih-Ting Cai and Coady Wing

We study whether GLP-1 initiation affects fertility and pregnancy outcomes among reproductive-age women with obesity using 2016-2024 Merative MarketScan Commercial claims. We document the size and composition of the GLP-1 candidate population, uptake among reproductive-age women, and medication use before, during, and after pregnancy. We then estimate effects on deliveries and related outcomes using an incident-user design and stacked cohort-specific difference-in-differences models with individual fixed effects to address endogenous treatment timing and fertility intent.

Exploring the Dynamics of "Incident to" Billing Practices: Implications of Full Practice Authority for Nurse Practitioners

with Megdalynn Fisher, D.T. Mitchell, and Coady Wing

Incident-to billing allows office services delivered by advanced practice providers to be billed under a supervising physician’s NPI, creating a payment wedge and potentially mismeasuring who produced care. Using 2020-2022 HealthJump EHR data from more than 4,000 office-based physician practice organizations, we link encounter-side and charge-side clinicians for the same hashed encounter to construct broad clinician mismatch and stricter advanced-practice-provider-to-physician mismatch measures. Among 10.7 million comparable billed office E&M records, broad mismatch affects about 14 percent of records annually and APP-to-physician mismatch affects 3.3 to 3.5 percent, with patterns concentrated across practices, persistent over time, increasing with provider-day throughput, and tied to meaningful dollar volume.

Scope of Practice Rules and Nurse Labor Supply

with Brian Phelan and Coady Wing

This study examines whether adoption of full nurse practitioner (NP) scope-of-practice laws expands the long-run primary care workforce by increasing entry into nursing and NP training pathways. Using 2000-2019 ACS, IPEDS, and National Council of State Boards of Nursing data, we estimate stacked difference-in-differences and event-study models to assess effects on college major choice, graduate degree attainment, nurse licensure, and RN exam candidates. Preliminary results indicate that full SOP adoption increases nursing majors, partly by drawing students away from life sciences, but provides limited evidence of increased graduate training or NP entry, suggesting that these policies expand RN supply more than the NP workforce.

Difference-in-Differences and Staggered Adoption Design (Book Chapter)

with Seth Freedman, Jennifer Mangano, and Coady Wing

This book chapter introduces difference-in-differences designs as a core tool for causal inference and explains why standard two-way fixed effects estimators can be misleading under staggered treatment timing or heterogeneous treatment effects. It develops the classic two-group, two-period framework and then extends the discussion to two modern alternatives: stacked difference-in-differences and imputation-based estimators. The chapter emphasizes the importance of separating estimation from aggregation across units and time, providing applied researchers with a framework for implementing credible, interpretable, and robust DID analyses.
